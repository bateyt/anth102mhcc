Whats the big deal?

Throughout most of human history, individual households and local communities practiced a hunting-gathering (also called hunting-foraging) subsistence strategy. In other words, they survived by a combination of hunting large and small game animals, fishing (if near riverine or marine environments), and gathering/foraging for wild plant foods.

Around 10,000 years ago (perhaps even earlier), though, some populations in the Levantthe region along the eastern Mediterranean in present-day countries like Turkey, Syria, Israel, and Jordanand Zagros Mountains (in present-day Iran) began the transition toward a new subsistence strategy: food production (i.e., agriculture and animal husbandry). Based on a large body of evidence, this larger region, often referred to as the Fertile Crescent, is thought to be where people first began (even unconsciously) the process of domesticating wild plant and animal species. Populations in other parts of the world (e.g., Africa, China, North and South America) also began independently cultivating domestic strains of indigenous, previously wild plant species.



Anthropologists/archaeologists in the early-/mid-20th century referred to this phenomenon the Neolithic Revolution. Today, we have a much more nuanced perspective of this complex process, such that it is now often referred to the Neolithic transition. Even still, the impact that domestication of plants and animals has had on human populations cannot be denied.

Archaeological data and historical evidence suggest that the rate of human population growth increased substantially (even small gains are meaningful when compared to zero or near-zero population growth) since the adoption of agriculture. Today, as the worlds population tops 7 billion (!), the ability to provide food for this ever-growing population is of prime importance.



In this lesson, we look at the evidence for the origins of agriculture, and our current interpretations of how and why humans began to produce, rather than just hunt/gather, their food. Also, we will consider the relationship between plant/animal domestication and changes in other social phenomena (settlement patterns, mortuary behavior, social complexity and inequality) observed in the archaeological record.
